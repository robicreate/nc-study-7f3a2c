📖 The Lecture (in plain English)

Everything from the 9 slides, re-explained with the "why," nursing connections, memory tricks, and fun facts.

1. What is an Element?

An element is a pure substance that cannot be broken down into anything simpler by a chemical reaction. Think of elements as the "letters of the alphabet" of all matter, every single thing around you is built from them.

  • There are 118 known elements. About 90 occur naturally; the other ~28 are made artificially in labs.
  • Each element has a 1- or 2-letter symbol (like Ca = calcium Fe = iron O = oxygen).
💡 The #1 rule everyone gets wrong: The first letter is ALWAYS capital, the second letter is ALWAYS lowercase. So cobalt is Co, not CO. That matters a lot, because CO (capital C, capital O) means carbon monoxide, a deadly gas! One is an element, the other is a compound.
Why two letters? There are only 26 letters but 118 elements, so single letters ran out fast. Some symbols even come from Latin names — that's why iron is Fe (ferrum), sodium is Na (natrium), and potassium is K (kalium). These trip up nursing students constantly because the symbol doesn't match the English word.
🎉 Fun fact: Only one element is named after a fictional character... none — but several are named after places (Californium, Americium) and scientists (Einsteinium, Curium). Seaborgium (Sg) on your slide is named after Glenn Seaborg, the only person who was alive when "his" element was named.

3. Elements in the Human Body

Living things selectively pull elements from their environment, so your body's "recipe" looks nothing like the Earth's crust. (Earth is loaded with silicon and aluminum; you are mostly oxygen, carbon, and hydrogen.) Your body is picky on purpose, it grabs exactly what it needs for life.

🩺 Nursing tie-in: This is the whole foundation of nutrition, IV fluids, and lab values. Every electrolyte panel you'll ever read (Na, K, Cl, Ca, Mg) is just "which elements, and how much" — exactly this slide.

4. Building-Block Elements (the big 4: CHON)

96% of your body mass is just four nonmetals:

C · H · O · N

Carbon · Hydrogen · Oxygen · Nitrogen

  • Oxygen + Hydrogen make water (H₂O) — the most abundant substance in your body.
  • C, H, O are in all 4 biological molecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids.
  • Nitrogen is the special one: it's in proteins and nucleic acids (DNA/RNA) only.
💡 Mnemonic: Remember "CHON" (sounds like "Shawn"). The big four building blocks = "Shawn" builds your body. And for the 4 biomolecules: "Carbs, Lipids, Proteins, Nucleic acids" → "CLiP-N".
🩺 Nursing tie-in: Nitrogen is why we track BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) for kidney function — protein breakdown releases nitrogen as urea. "Nitrogen lives in protein" is a fact you'll use on the floor.
🎉 Fun fact: By mass you're ~65% oxygen, but most of that is locked inside water, not the air you breathe. You're basically a very organized puddle.

5. Major Minerals — Macronutrients

These make up 0.1% to 2% of body mass. You need at least 100 mg of each per day. Memorize them by where they work:

WhereMinerals
Body fluids (electrolytes)K Potassium, Na Sodium, Cl Chlorine
Proteins in musclesMg Magnesium, S Sulfur
Teeth & bonesCa Calcium, P Phosphorus

Bonus: Phosphorus (P) is also in nucleic acids (the backbone of DNA/RNA).

🩺 Nursing tie-in: "Fluid minerals" = your electrolytes. Low potassium (hypokalemia) can stop a heart; sodium controls fluid balance; calcium runs muscle contraction and clotting. This single table is the seed of half of med-surg nursing.
💡 Trick: The "100 mg club" — if you need ≥100 mg/day, it's a major mineral. Less than that = trace (next section).

6. Trace Elements — Micronutrients

Each is under 0.1% of body mass, and you need 15 mg or less per day. Tiny amounts, huge jobs. Sort them by type:

TypeElements
MetalsCr, Co, Cu, Fe, Mn, Mo, Ni, Zn
NonmetalsF, I, Se
MetalloidsAs, B, Si
💡 What's a metalloid? An "in-between" element that acts part metal, part nonmetal. The 3 here are A.B.S. → Arsenic, Boron, Silicon. (Think "ABS brakes" — in between stop and go.)
🎉 Fun fact: Arsenic is a famous poison, yet your body uses a trace of it. The dose makes the poison — a rule that runs through all of pharmacology.

7. What the Trace Elements Actually Do

ElementJob
Fe IronIn hemoglobin (carries O₂ in red blood cells) and myoglobin (stores O₂ in muscle)
Zn ZincIn enzymes of the liver and kidneys
I IodineNeeded for thyroid function
🩺 Nursing tie-in (this is gold):
  • Low iron → anemia. Less hemoglobin = less oxygen delivery = fatigue, pale skin, shortness of breath. You'll see this constantly.
  • Low iodine → thyroid problems (goiter, hypothyroidism). It's why table salt is "iodized."
  • Hemoglobin vs. myoglobin: hemo = highway (carries O₂ in blood), myo = muscle storage. Easy to mix up on exams.
💡 The slide says "look up the rest on your phone." Here are the high-yield ones professors test: Cu (copper) helps iron form hemoglobin; Se (selenium) is an antioxidant; Cr (chromium) helps insulin/blood sugar; Co (cobalt) is the center of vitamin B12; F (fluoride) hardens teeth.

8. Compounds & Reading Chemical Formulas

A compound is a pure substance made by chemically combining 2 or more elements. We describe them with chemical formulas built from element symbols.

The 3 rules for counting atoms

  • Subscript = how many of the element right before it. H₂O = 2 H, 1 O. (No number means 1.)
  • Parentheses ( ) group atoms, and the subscript outside multiplies everything inside. Mg(OH)₂ = 1 Mg, then (O,H)×2 = 2 O and 2 H.
  • Multiply, then add up each element. Mg₃(PO₄)₂ = 3 Mg, P: 1×2 = 2, O: 4×2 = 8.
FormulaAtom count
H₂O2 H, 1 O
CO1 C, 1 O (carbon monoxide)
CO₂1 C, 2 O (carbon dioxide)
C₆H₁₂O₆6 C, 12 H, 6 O (glucose!)
Na₃(PO₄)3 Na, 1 P, 4 O
Mg(OH)₂1 Mg, 2 O, 2 H
Mg₃(PO₄)₂3 Mg, 2 P, 8 O
The "why" behind the parentheses: The (PO₄) group is a phosphate — a team of atoms that stays together. The ×2 means "I need two whole phosphate teams." That's why you multiply both the P and the O. Distribute it like math: 2 × (P₁O₄) = P₂O₈.
🩺 Nursing tie-in: C₆H₁₂O₆ is glucose — blood sugar, the thing every diabetic patient and every IV "D5W" bag is about. CO₂ is what you breathe out and a key part of blood pH. CO (carbon monoxide) binds hemoglobin 200× tighter than oxygen — that's why it's deadly. The difference between CO and CO₂ is literally one subscript.
💡 Watch out: A subscript only counts the atom immediately before it. In CO₂ the 2 belongs to O only, not C. Slow down and read left to right.

⭐ The 60-Second Recap

  • Element = can't be broken down; 118 total; 1–2 letter symbol (1st capital, 2nd lowercase).
  • 96% of you = CHON (Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen). N is only in proteins + nucleic acids.
  • Major minerals (≥100 mg/day): fluids = K, Na, Cl · muscle = Mg, S · bones/teeth = Ca, P.
  • Trace elements (≤15 mg/day): Fe (hemoglobin/myoglobin), Zn (liver/kidney enzymes), I (thyroid).
  • Compound = 2+ elements combined; subscript counts the atom before it; parentheses multiply everything inside.

Set A — Symbols & Names

1. Give the symbols for: sodium, potassium, iron, calcium, iodine.Na, K, Fe, Ca, I
2. Name these elements: Au, Pb, Hg, Sn, Cu.Gold, Lead, Mercury, Tin, Copper
3. A student writes "co" for cobalt and "CO" elsewhere. What's wrong, and what does CO actually mean?Cobalt must be written Co (capital C, lowercase o). CO = the compound carbon monoxide. Capitalization changes the meaning entirely.
4. Which of these symbols come from Latin names? Fe, O, Na, Cl, Ag, K.Fe (ferrum), Na (natrium), Ag (argentum), K (kalium). O and Cl come from their English/Greek names.

Set B — Body Composition

5. Name the four "building block" elements and the percent of body mass they make up.Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen (CHON) — about 96%.
6. Which building-block element is found ONLY in proteins and nucleic acids (not in carbs or lipids)?Nitrogen (N).
7. Classify each as MAJOR mineral or TRACE element: calcium, iron, sodium, iodine, phosphorus, zinc.Major: calcium, sodium, phosphorus. Trace: iron, iodine, zinc.
8. Your daily need for a mineral is 250 mg. Major or trace? How do you know?Major — major minerals require at least 100 mg/day; trace elements require 15 mg or less.
9. Match the mineral group to its location: (K, Na, Cl) / (Mg, S) / (Ca, P).K, Na, Cl → body fluids. Mg, S → proteins in muscles. Ca, P → teeth and bones.

Set C — Functions (the "why")

10. What does iron do, and what's the difference between hemoglobin and myoglobin?Iron is needed for both. Hemoglobin carries oxygen in red blood cells; myoglobin stores oxygen in muscle.
11. A patient has low iodine. Which organ/system is affected?The thyroid — iodine is needed for thyroid function.
12. Why does low iron cause fatigue and shortness of breath? (Reason it out.)Less iron → less hemoglobin → blood carries less oxygen → tissues get less O₂ → fatigue and breathlessness (anemia).

Set D — Counting Atoms in Formulas

13. How many of each atom in CO₂?1 carbon, 2 oxygen.
14. How many of each atom in C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose)?6 carbon, 12 hydrogen, 6 oxygen.
15. How many of each atom in Mg(OH)₂?1 magnesium, 2 oxygen, 2 hydrogen.
16. How many of each atom in Na₃(PO₄)?3 sodium, 1 phosphorus, 4 oxygen.
17. ⭐ Challenge: How many of each atom in Mg₃(PO₄)₂? Show the multiplication.3 Mg; phosphorus 1×2 = 2 P; oxygen 4×2 = 8 O. → 3 Mg, 2 P, 8 O.
18. ⭐ Challenge: How many TOTAL atoms are in C₆H₁₂O₆?6 + 12 + 6 = 24 atoms.
19. ⭐ Challenge: Write the atom counts for Ca₃(PO₄)₂ (calcium phosphate — the mineral in your bones!).3 calcium; P: 1×2 = 2; O: 4×2 = 8. → 3 Ca, 2 P, 8 O.
